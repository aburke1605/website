Education

In 2016 I completed three A-levels at school in Derry, Ireland, achieving A* \((\geq 90 \%)\) grades in Mathematics, Physics, and Chemistry, before moving to Manchester for university.

I initially spent four years (2016-2020) at the University of Manchester, gaining a first class \((83 \%)\) integrated-Masters degree in Physics, and a top-10 finish in my year group. This was followed by a further four years (2020-2024) spent researching and obtaining obtaining a doctorate in the field of particle physics.

Professional work

I currently work for the Civil Engineering department at the University of Liverpool, leading a small software development team.

We work in partnership with renewable energy, energy storage and electronics companies in a government-funded project worth (over) £1.4m, with aims to design and realise a mini-grid energy storage system for use in Sub-Saharan Africa and Ukraine.

The specific role of my team is to produce a remote monitoring and diagnostic system for the mini-grid and its individual removable battery modules.

This encompases everything from the firmware of the microcontrollers interfaced with each battery module, to the frontend of the monitoring website portal and all of the infrastructure and networking in between.

Prior to this, I briefly worked in a Postdoctoral role as a continuation of my PhD research with the University of Manchester and CERN.

Simulation of particle physics

The simulation below (of a general-purpose particle detector at the Large Hadron Collider) was my first large-scale project with C++. It shows a slice through a detector such as ATLAS, illustrating how different kinds of particles interact with different shells of the detector, using a random number generator to simulate real life fundamental physics. I built this as the final assignment in my undergraduate course, Object-Oriented Programming in C++. It makes use of many advanced C++ features, including smart pointers, lambdas, polymorphism and templating.
